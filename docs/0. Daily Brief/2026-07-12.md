## Deep Dive: Why the Strait of Hormuz Keeps Dragging the World to the Brink

Every serious flare-up with Iran eventually comes back to a single strip of water. The Strait of Hormuz is roughly 33 km wide at its narrowest, separating Iran from Oman and the UAE, and it is the only sea route out of the Persian Gulf. Around a fifth of the world's oil — some 20 million barrels a day — plus a large share of global liquefied natural gas (LNG — natural gas cooled to liquid for shipping) passes through it. That geography gives Tehran outsized leverage: it cannot win a conventional war against the US and Israel, but it can choke a waterway the global economy depends on.

That is exactly the dynamic that played out this week. Iran has never formally "closed" the strait for long, but it doesn't need to — attacking a few tankers, laying mines, or imposing "tolls" and pre-approved routes is enough to spike insurance costs, reroute traffic, and rattle oil markets. Reporting suggests Iran even lost track of some of its own mines, complicating any clean reopening. The economic pain lands hardest on Asia: China, India, Japan, and South Korea are the strait's biggest customers, which is why this week also saw ripple effects like China's move to restrict helium exports.

Alternatives exist but are thin. Overland pipelines through Saudi Arabia and the UAE can bypass only about 9 million barrels a day — under half the strait's flow — and the Red Sea detour is itself exposed to Houthi attacks. Navies have stepped in: the US has run mine-clearing and escort operations, and India launched Operation Urja Suraksha to shepherd its tankers through the Gulf of Oman. But escorting even a handful of ships a day sustainably ties up large numbers of warships, which is why a negotiated, durable reopening — not a military one — is what everyone is really chasing.

The bottom line for readers: watch the strait as the real barometer of the Iran war. Ceasefire language about Iran using its "best efforts" for safe passage left deliberate ambiguity over tolls and control, and that ambiguity is now the fault line. Whether the shooting stops will be decided less by rhetoric than by whether ships move freely through those 33 km of water.
