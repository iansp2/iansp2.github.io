### Content of the Day — Tool Spotlight: Certipy

If NetExec is your protocol multitool, **Certipy** is your dedicated wrecking ball for **Active Directory Certificate Services (AD CS)**. It rolls enumeration, certificate requests, PKINIT auth, shadow credentials, CA abuse, and relay into a single Python tool — replacing the older Certify + Rubeus + ForgeCert dance. Reach for it the moment you see a CA in a domain, because AD CS misconfigs (the ESC1–ESC16 family) are some of the most reliable "user → Domain Admin" primitives in modern AD.

The workflow is `find → req → auth`. Start with `certipy find -u user@domain.local -p 'pass' -dc-ip <DC> -vulnerable -stdout` — it enumerates CAs and templates, flags exploitable ESC conditions, and can emit BloodHound-ingestible output so cert edges show up alongside your ACL graph. The bread-and-butter kill is **ESC1**: a template that permits client authentication *and* lets the enrollee supply an arbitrary subject (`msPKI-Certificate-Name-Flag: ENROLLEE_SUPPLIES_SUBJECT`). You request a cert impersonating a privileged user — `certipy req -u user@domain.local -p 'pass' -ca 'CORP-CA' -template 'VulnTemplate' -upn administrator@domain.local` — then turn that PFX into a TGT and NT hash with `certipy auth -pfx administrator.pfx -dc-ip <DC>` (PKINIT, with UnPAC-the-hash handing you the hash for free). Instant DA, no password crack, no LSASS touch.

Certipy's other modes map cleanly to techniques you've seen this week: `shadow auto` writes `msDS-KeyCredentialLink` for **Shadow Credentials**; `relay` performs **ESC8** — coerce a DC (yesterday's piece) and relay NTLM straight to the CA's web-enrollment endpoint (`/certsrv/`) to mint a machine cert; and `ca`/`forge` cover CA config tampering and **golden certificate** forgery once you've looted the CA private key. In MITRE terms this is **T1649 (Steal or Forge Authentication Certificates)** feeding **T1550.003 (Pass-the-Ticket)**, with ESC8 bridging into **T1557 (AiTM relay)**.

This is squarely CPTS/CRTP territory rather than classic OSCP scope — certificate abuse barely appears in the old PWK material — but it's everywhere in real engagements and lab retirees. You'll hit textbook ESC1/ESC8/shadow-cred chains on HTB **Authority**, **Escape**, and several Proving Grounds AD boxes. Learn to read `certipy find` output fluently and the ADCS half of BloodHound stops being scary.

### One Thing to Try

`certipy find -u 'user@domain.local' -p 'Password123' -dc-ip 10.10.10.10 -vulnerable -stdout` — enumerate the CA and hunt for an ESC1 template (client-auth EKU + `ENROLLEE_SUPPLIES_SUBJECT`); if you spot one, you're one `req`/`auth` away from impersonating a Domain Admin.
