## Deep Dive: The 2026 Iran War and the Fight for the Strait of Hormuz

The current fighting traces to 28 February 2026, when the US and Israel launched surprise strikes on Iranian military, government and nuclear-linked sites, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei and other senior officials. Iran retaliated with missile and drone salvos against US bases and Gulf partners and moved to close the Strait of Hormuz — the narrow chokepoint through which a large share of the world's seaborne oil transits ([Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/event/2026-Iran-war)).

Since then the conflict has repeatedly cycled between escalation and truce. The US mounted an air campaign to reopen the strait, imposed a naval blockade of Iran in April, and — after Pakistani-mediated talks — reached a June memorandum of understanding meant to end the war and restore free navigation. That framework never fully held: Iran has pressed to control traffic through Hormuz and levy fees, prompting renewed US strikes whenever ships were attacked ([Wikipedia: 2026 Strait of Hormuz crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2026_Strait_of_Hormuz_crisis)).

The key players now extend well beyond Washington and Tehran. Iran is led by Mojtaba Khamenei following his father's death. Gulf states — Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, Jordan and Saudi Arabia — host US forces and have become targets, pulling them reluctantly into the crossfire. Pakistan has acted as mediator, while Israel continues parallel operations, including in southern Lebanon against Hezbollah ([CBS News](https://www.cbsnews.com/live-updates/iran-war-trump-strait-of-hormuz-attacks-persian-gulf/)).

The stakes are global: sustained disruption at Hormuz reverberates through energy prices, shipping and aviation, and each strike on Gulf infrastructure raises the risk of a wider regional war. This week's twin developments — American combat deaths in Jordan and Iran's suspension of the MoU — mark the framework's most serious breakdown yet, even as officials on both sides insist diplomacy is not dead ([NPR](https://www.npr.org/2026/07/18/nx-s1-5898916/us-iran-escalate-strikes)).

What to watch: whether the MoU's suspension tips the war back into open, sustained fighting; the pace of US strikes and Gulf casualties; and whether mediators can reopen talks before the confrontation broadens.
